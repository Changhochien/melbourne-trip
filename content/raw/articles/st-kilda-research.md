# St Kilda Research

## What It Is
Melbourne's most famous seaside suburb — 6km south of the CBD. A beachside community with a grand pier, a faded fun park history, persistent glamour, and excellent food. Reached by tram 96 (Sydney Road to St Kilda — part of the world's longest tram route).

## Key Facts
- **Tram:** Route 96 — runs from Sydney Road in the north through the CBD to St Kilda
- **Distance from CBD:** ~6km south
- **Journey time:** ~25 min from Flinders Street by tram
- **Beach:** Grey sand, Southern Ocean water (cold year-round, 14–17°C even in summer); not a swimming beach for most due to rough conditions
- **July water temperature:** ~13°C — too cold to swim

## Key Landmarks

### St Kilda Pier (1880s)
Historic pier with a 1920s Kelpie kiosk at the end. Penguin colony below the breakwater (little penguins, not the Phillip Island variety — these are smaller and free to view at the pier itself).

### Luna Park
Operating since 1912. The entrance is the iconic mouth of a giant cartoon face. Tickets are per-ride, not unlimited. The historic wooden roller coaster (The Great Scenic Railway) is one of only a few left in the world.

### Acland Street
The eat street — Jewish bakeries (Dagmar's is famous), the St Kilda Cake House, and a cluster of good-value restaurants. Slightly touristy but genuinely good.

### The Esplanade Hotel ("The Espy")
Legendary punk and rock venue with multiple bars, live music, and history. One of Melbourne's great institution venues.

## History
St Kilda was Melbourne's playground in the late 19th and early 20th centuries — a beach resort accessible by tram. The Palais theatre and Luna Park date from this era. The fun park never fully succeeded but the neighbourhood retained its slightly faded glamour.

## July Specifics
- Early sunsets (~5:15 PM) make afternoon pier walks at sunset ideal timing
- Beach is not appealing for swimming but good for walks
- The Espy and Acland Street restaurants are ideal for winter evenings
