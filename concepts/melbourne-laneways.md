# Melbourne Laneways

Melbourne's defining urban feature — a network of narrow pedestrian lanes hidden behind the main street grid, filled with street art, hole-in-the-wall cafes, bars, and boutiques. The product of Melbourne's growth pattern: building behind established frontages.

## Why Laneways Exist
When Melbourne was surveyed in 1837, the Hoddle Grid included rear access lanes for deliveries and servicing. These became the city's social arteries as foot traffic migrated indoors.

## Key Laneways

### Hosier Lane
The most famous street art lane. Continuous rotation of paste-ups, tags, and pieces. Near [[flinders-street-station]]. Best seen in morning light.

### AC/DC Lane
Named for the rock band, home to the band's mural. Chain-metal decor.

### Duckboard Place
A short lane off Flinders Lane — quieter, more design-focused.

### Rankins Lane
Narrow and blink-and-miss-it.

### Presgrave Place
A tiny pink-painted pocket near Elizabeth Street.

### Talinga Lane
Off Collins, subtle.

## Street Art Culture
The City of Melbourne permits street art in specific laneways. Hosier Lane is the most photographed. Tour guides do laneway tours — worth it for the context.
